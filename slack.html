Slack for Android Redesign

Yet Another Unsolicited Redesign Concept
(Not created by, affiliated with, or supported by Slack Technologies, Inc.)

Introduction

Slack has always been one of my favorite apps: They are a great example of my personal belief that experiences, even professional or serious ones, should be designed with "delightfulness" in mind. Slack makes heavy usage of nice graphics as well as friendly (and snarky) content text and features designed solely to encourage fun.

To be honest, I am personally not a fan of unsolicited redesigns: I find it incredibly difficult to redesign existing services understanding the factors that caused the reasons their designers made it the way it is: there are often a lot of factors, decisions made, and compromises that I don't know about. Because of this, I'm trying to make this project less of a "redesign" and more of "little touches" I think would make the app more enjoyable to use.

I have also used this project to explore working and being consistent with an existing visual style, something I have not really gotten the opportunity to do in most of my personal projects. I tried to show this in everything from the redesign itself to this webpage itself.

User Research

As a heavy Slack user myself and a strong believer in mobile apps being consistent with the rest of operating system's style, there were quite a few things about the Android app I wished to touch up. I also decided to get thoughts from other users as well to help make sure this wasn't just me making my own personal "ideal" version of slack.

I interviewed about a dozen of my friends who were heavy slack users to get their opinion on the slack experience on mobile. Many of them discussed blah blah one common trend was the lack of consistency between the app compared to the desktop version.

This mostly meant the lack of being able to find (or have) certain features and being confused because of having interfaces that were similar enough to remind them it's the same app yet still had some major differences in terms of where certain items were laid out.

The Process

I, like many people, start off most of my designs in a sketchbook: I prefer to work with dotted notebooks because they provide a nice grid without the grid getting in the way of the drawings too much.

I like to prototype interfaces with what I call "thumbnails," very small and rough drawings of interfaces. I like doing it this way because it prevents me from getting attached to certain interfaces/screens because they're so small and quick, and it strongly encourages me to think about the bigger elements because there's not enough room for the smaller visual details. It also makes it a lot easier to think about interaction and how the screens connect to each other.

About the same time as the sketches I like to quickly mockup the interfaces and designs in Sketch. I like to keep my process and all my screens (duplicating screens whenever I want to try something new) as well as my inspiration and reference material (in this case, the old app's screenshots as well as the desktop app) around so I can look at them all fairly easily.

Analysis

I spent the first part of this exploring the current state of Slack's Android app and how it corresponds to the parts of the desktop app: where menus and menu items appear and how they're connected to each other.

The Northeast

Although the right side of the action bar somewhat seems to correspond to the top right of the desktop app, I do find it somewhat awkward. The action bar on Android doesn't really show the channel name but rather the Team's name, which is in the sidebar on the desktop app. In addition, the options in the "settings" aren't completely complete (in the Android app, most of the remaining options occur in the menu on the sidebar, which will be shown shortly.)

The Left Sidebar

The sidebar is a bit different: instead of displaying the team information (which is instead on the main "action bar," the sidebar has a menu with some fairly abritary contents (e.g. mentions, starred items) which correspond to things on the top left part of the menu in the Desktop app.

The Direct Messages option is kind of out of place in my opinion: in the desktop app it can be accessed by clicking on the Direct Messages title on the sidebar, below the list of channels.

A Place for Messages

A New Home

The "main" screen of the app where the messages are displayed has been slightly redesigned. The Action bar has been made a bit shorter and how highlights the name of the channel to be more consistent with the desktop app. (The Team Name has been moved over to the sidebar, more on this later.)

The menu on the side now contains many of the other options found there on the desktop app, especially the "recent mentions" and "starred items." I chose to move these two over not only for consistency but also so that I remove them from the menu on the left side bar to make room for the Team Name/settings.

The Far Left

The sidebar has been slightly redesigned to match the sidebar on the desktop app. The previous menu/title has been replaced with the Team Name.

The "Team Switcher" has been replaced with the "Snooze" button. Although the "Team Switcher" was quite convenient, I chose to hide it within the Team menu because even though it was quite convenient, even as someone who was a part of many teams I didn't find myself switching enough on mobile that it warranted it's own dedicated button in prime real estate, plus hiding it matched the interface of the desktop app better.

(The empty fields next to locks on the sidebar are me censoring the name of our team's private channels.)

Sidebar Menus

The previous "Home|Mentions|Starred|Direct Messages" menu has been replaced with one that matches the items seen in the Desktop app better. (Mentions and Starred Items have been moved to the other sidebar, where they seem to fit more consistently.)

The Left menu now contains items relevant to the team, such as Team Directory and team switcher. In addition, "Customize Slack," one of my favorite features of Slack that I often miss on mobile has been added.

The right menu conveniently contains some options for snoozing notifications, something that I find myself doing a lot, especially from mobile.

Direct Messages

In the current app, direct messages can be accessed via a menu option at the top. This loads the direct messages screen, which is somehow awkwardly put in the sidebar. This is not found in the Desktop or IOS app and somewhat surprised me.

Redesigned Messages

I chose to make the new direct messages view accessible in a similar way to the way it is found in the other apps: by scrolling down and tapping on the "direct messages" word.

This would then open up the list of previous messages in its own dedicated screen, which should fade away as soon as the user picks who they wanted to message, which feels a lot less awkward than putting it in the sidebar (although I do somewhat feel the intuition for putting it there in the first place).

Channel Info and Actions

The merged "Channel Info" and "Channel Actions" screen, accessible by tapping on a channel name, although nice looking, doesn't match that well with both the rest of the app as well as with the Android guidelines and the Desktop app (it's supposed to correspond to the "info" sidebar.) Because of this, I redesigned it to visually match better.

In addition, I have added in the ability to view pinned posts (collapsible in case there are many of them). Viewing a Pinned Post should close the Channel Info window then go to the post. Although whether or not to allow viewing of pinned posts on mobile is an interesting design decision to think about, I found myself, from personal on the go usage, that being unable to view pinned posts on mobile causes issues quite often.

Customize Slack

One of the reasons I like slack a lot and believe it does so well is because of its friendliness and ability to be plain fun, partially due to the ability to customize it.

Although these feature aren't as a priority and don't appear on mobile at the moment, they would definitely be a nice addition: I have many times wished to be able to add new emojis from mobile or see what someone set as the trigger to a slackbot response.

I spent a bit of time deciding whether to make the layout tabbed like the website or more "listy" like the rest of the app: I eventually settled on the first option because it works much better when there are many customizations, which should be fairly normal for many teams.
